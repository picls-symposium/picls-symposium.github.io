Speakers
Prof. Giulio Cossu

Institute of Inflammation and Repair, University of Manchester ...

Giulio Cossu is recognized for his pioneering work on skeletal myogenesis and for the first cell therapy trial with stem cells for the treatment of muscular dystrophy. He is an author of 200 peer-reviewed publications and receives funding from many national and international agencies, such as Telethon, Duchenne Parent Project, Human Frontiers Science Organization, European Community, European Research Council and others. He originally showed that neural tube an subsequently the dorsal ectoderm activate through different Wnts, distinct myogenic programs in epaxial and hypaxial somitic progenitors, a notion now in developmental biology textbooks. In collaboration with M. Buckingham laboratory, Giulio elucidated the hierarchy of different myogenic regulatory factors. His work led to the identification of a bone-marrow derived circulating myogenic progenitor cell population in adult mice, termed mesoangioblasts, that are able to proliferate in vitro and contribute to mesoderm tissues upon transplantation. Mesoangioblasts were used for the first successful cell therapy for muscular dystrophy in mice and dogs. After characterization of human mesoangioblasts, these cells were used for a “first in man” phase I/II clinical trial based upon allo-transplantation of donor mesoangioblasts from an HLA-identical donor in patients affected by Duchenne muscular dystrophy. The trial started in March 2011 and is currently in progress with encouraging results. Ongoing research focuses on the development of Human Artificial Chromosomes encoding the dystrophin locus which was tested successfully in dystrophic mice. In parallel iPS cell derived mesoangioblasts have been developed and tested for models of muscular dystrophy where the endogenous number of progenitors may be exhausted or insufficient. Overall , Giulio's research activity is characterized by the unique ability to combine work in developmental biology with cell therapy, successfully translating results obtained in mammalian embryos into clinical protocols in patients.

About

The College of Life Sciences’ PhD Association (PiCLS) within the University of Dundee was established in 2008 to allow networking between PhD students in different subject areas. In 2010 the PiCLS symposium was introduced and was such a great success that the event is now an annual symposium run entirely by students.
